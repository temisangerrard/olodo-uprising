Olodo Uprising: A Behavioral Archetype Assessment Tool
Theoretical Foundations, Methodology, and Societal Outcome Modeling

Temisan Gerrard

Independent Researcher

Version 1.0 — June 2026

Abstract

Olodo Uprising is a behavioral archetype assessment tool designed to model how individuals would behave if replicated 10,000 times in a shared society. The core question is simple: Does your presence make society better or worse? This paper presents the theoretical foundations, psychometric design principles, and methodology behind the assessment, drawing on five decades of research in personality psychology, moral foundations theory, behavioral economics, and social identity theory. We identify five behavioral archetypes — Builder, Sage, Skeptic, Opportunist, and Intellectual — each with distinct behavioral signatures and measurable societal outcomes. Questions are grounded in real Nigerian social tensions to bypass impression management and reveal authentic behavioral patterns. We further present a Societal Outcome Model that maps archetype distributions to predicted societal states, enabling both individual assessment and collective simulation. The tool is deployed as an open-source web application at olodo-uprising.pages.dev.

Keywords: behavioral assessment, moral foundations, social identity, cognitive dissonance, personality archetypes, agent-based modeling, societal simulation, Nigerian society

1. Introduction

1.1 The Simulation Hypothesis

Personality assessment has traditionally focused on self-reported traits, preferences, and beliefs. However, these measures often fail to predict actual behavior in real-world contexts (Funder, 2001). Olodo Uprising takes a different approach: rather than asking "What do you believe?" or "What would you prefer?", we ask "What would you do?"

The core concept is a thought experiment: If we placed 10,000 copies of you in a radius, what kind of society would emerge? This reveals the connection between individual behavior and collective outcomes. The premise draws on agent-based modeling traditions (Epstein, 2006) where macro-level patterns emerge from micro-level behavioral rules, but applies it to personality assessment rather than economic simulation.

Consider the implications:

  • 10,000 Builders → Industrialized, functioning infrastructure, merit-based systems
  • 10,000 Sages → Educated, thoughtful discourse, but potential paralysis by analysis
  • 10,000 Skeptics → Critical thinking culture, but potential cynicism and inaction
  • 10,000 Opportunists → Extractive society, corruption, zero-sum competition
  • 10,000 Intellectuals → Sophisticated rationalizations for dishonesty, educated corruption

The tool doesn't measure what people think they should do. It measures what they would actually do when faced with genuine trade-offs between competing values.

1.2 Why Nigeria?

Nigeria provides a particularly rich context for studying behavioral archetypes. The country faces a unique combination of challenges: ethnic and religious fragmentation (over 250 ethnic groups, roughly equal Muslim-Christian split), resource curse dynamics where oil wealth has created extractive institutions rather than inclusive development, an educational paradox where high levels of formal education coexist with widespread institutional failure, and a youth bulge (over 60% under 25) creating both opportunity and instability.

These tensions make Nigeria an ideal laboratory for studying how individual behavioral tendencies aggregate into societal outcomes. The questions are grounded in real Nigerian social contexts — religious violence, ethnic tribalism, political corruption, intellectual hypocrisy — but the underlying psychological mechanisms are universal.

2. Theoretical Foundations

2.1 Intelligence as a Tool, Not a Moral Compass

A central insight of Olodo Uprising is that higher cognitive ability enables more sophisticated rationalization, not better moral judgment. This challenges the common assumption that education correlates with ethical behavior.

Kahan et al. (2017) conducted a landmark experiment demonstrating that cognitive ability serves motivated reasoning rather than correcting it. When data were presented neutrally, high-numeracy subjects performed better at statistical reasoning. However, when the same data were framed as gun-control results, high-numeracy subjects became more polarized — not less. Cognitive ability is not a corrective to motivated reasoning; it is a resource for motivated reasoning.

Implication: The "Intellectual" archetype is not defined by low intelligence. It is defined by high intelligence + low truthfulness + low accountability. These individuals use their education to construct sophisticated justifications for dishonest behavior. A lawyer who calls a successful entrepreneur a "failure" while unable to generate equivalent income is not unintelligent — they are using their legal training to construct narratives that mask personal inadequacy.

2.2 Dark Triad Traits and Education

Research on the Dark Triad — narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — reveals that these traits predict dishonesty independently of education level (Paulhus & Williams, 2002). Narcissism drives grandiosity and entitlement; Machiavellianism drives strategic manipulation ("ends justify means") and is the most consistent predictor of academic dishonesty; psychopathy drives impulsivity and lack of empathy.

Critical insight: Educated people with Dark Triad traits are more dangerous because they can construct convincing rationalizations and evade detection. Their education provides vocabulary and frameworks to make unethical behavior appear principled. Nigerian academics have been incorporated into what Omotola (CODESRIA) calls the "corruption enterprise" — the academy's crisis mirrors the state's crisis, with intellectuals offering "insincere advice and policy options to government so as to advance selfish interests."

Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory (2012) identifies six innate moral intuitions that form the basis of ethical reasoning across cultures (Table 1).

As Haidt puts it: "The intuitive dog wags the rational tail." Educated Nigerians may genuinely believe they are acting morally — they are using Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity foundations to justify corruption that serves their ethnic or religious in-group.

2.4 Cognitive Dissonance and Motivated Reasoning

Festinger's (1957) cognitive dissonance theory describes the psychological tension that arises when beliefs conflict with behavior or new evidence. People resolve this tension through rationalization — adjusting attitudes to restore consistency. Critically, people with greater cognitive ability can be more susceptible to rationalizing mistakes because they are confident in their reasoning prowess.

Kunda (1990) demonstrated that people do not reason neutrally — they reason toward desired conclusions. More extensive processing combined with directional goals produces more extreme biased conclusions. System 2 (analytical thinking) becomes "a skilled lawyer" — negotiating away tension between evidence and prior beliefs rather than evaluating evidence objectively (Kahneman, 2011).

Bandura's (1999) moral disengagement theory identifies mechanisms by which people decouple internal moral standards from behavior: moral justification, euphemistic labeling, displacement of responsibility, dehumanization, and attribution of blame. Research shows that intelligence positively predicts moral justification — smarter people are better at constructing moral-sounding excuses (Detert, Treviño, & Sweitzer, 2008).

2.5 Social Identity Theory and Tribal Behavior

Tajfel and Turner's (1979) social identity theory explains how group memberships shape behavior through three processes: categorization, identification, and comparison (favoring in-group). Key tribal mechanisms include in-group favoritism (favoring group members even without competition), out-group homogeneity (perceiving out-group members as "all the same"), and parochial altruism (generosity within the group paired with hostility toward outsiders).

In Nigeria, ethnic, religious, and political identities are fused in the north — making conflict inherently ethnoreligious. In the south, identities are more separate. Strategic deployment of ethnicity by elites fragments potential class-based challenges to dominance (Osaghae, 1998). Social identity does not disappear with education. Educated people simply find more sophisticated ways to defend their tribe.

2.6 Behavioral Economics: Self-Concept Maintenance

Mazar, Amir, and Ariely's (2008) theory of self-concept maintenance explains why most people are dishonest in predictable ways. Most people are dishonest enough to profit but honest enough to maintain a positive self-view. The magnitude of dishonesty is small (approximately 16–20% of maximum possible gain) and is insensitive to external costs/benefits but sensitive to psychological and contextual factors.

Two key mechanisms operate: (1) Attention to Moral Standards — when people's attention is drawn to honesty standards, cheating drops to zero; (2) Categorization Malleability — when actions can be reinterpreted self-servingly, dishonesty increases. Dana, Weber, and Kuang (2007) demonstrated "moral wiggle room" — people exploit uncertainty to behave selfishly while maintaining plausible deniability through strategic ignorance and diffused responsibility. Gino and Pierce (2013) found that people cheat more when others benefit from their cheating, creating a "lure of unethical actions that benefit others."

3. Design Principles

3.1 "Would Do" Not "Should Do"

"Should do" questions capture normative knowledge — what people think they ought to do — revealing social desirability bias rather than actual behavior. We frame questions as "What would you actually do?" — behavioral tendency questions reveal character better than normative knowledge. For example: ❌ "Should you report corruption?" (Everyone says yes) vs. ✅ "You discover corruption in your community. You:" (Forces actual behavioral choice).

3.2 Force Genuine Trade-offs

Questions with obviously "right" answers do not reveal character. We create scenarios with conflicting values — loyalty vs. honesty, fairness vs. compassion, truth vs. social harmony. Example: "Your ethnic group's historical narrative is inaccurate. You:" — Correct the record (Truth > Loyalty), Stay quiet (Loyalty > Truth), or Write sophisticated justifications (Intellectual dishonesty).

3.3 Create Moral Wiggle Room

People answer based on aspirational self-image, not actual behavior. We design scenarios where selfish choices can be rationalized by introducing ambiguity, intermediary steps, or "benefiting others" justifications. Example: "A politician offers you funding for your community project, but the money comes from corruption. You:" — Refuse (Clear integrity), Accept because the community needs this (Self-serving altruism), or Accept and write op-eds defending the politician (Sophisticated rationalization).

3.4 Bypass Impression Management

People try to present their best self. We use: covert items (don't make obvious what trait is being assessed), forgiving language (normalize undesirable behaviors), time pressure (quick responses bypass impression management), and consistency checks (same construct measured different ways).

3.5 Multiple Measurement Strategies

Single questions are unreliable. We measure the same construct across multiple contexts: different ambiguity levels, different stakes, different social contexts, and different moral foundations. Truthfulness, for example, is measured across religious violence (Q1), ethnic hiring (Q2), political corruption (Q12), and intellectual dishonesty (Q22).

3.6 Detect Faking in Real-Time

We include overt and covert versions of the same construct, measure response consistency, use within-person standardization, and include "ideal worker" detection items. If someone answers "Always tell the truth" to 20 questions, they are either lying or not paying attention.

4. Archetype Definitions

4.1 The Builder

Core Traits: High initiative, accountable, practical, results-driven.

Behavioral Signature: Takes action when others complain; owns mistakes publicly; builds systems, not just opinions; values outcomes over process.

Societal Outcome (100%): Industrialized Nigeria in a decade. Infrastructure gets built, problems get solved, merit-based systems emerge.

Measurement: Questions about initiative (Q9, Q15, Q25), accountability (Q10, Q16, Q21), and practical problem-solving (Q3, Q6, Q18).

4.2 The Sage

Core Traits: High knowledge, thoughtful, analytical, principled.

Behavioral Signature: Seeks evidence before forming opinions; changes mind when presented with new evidence; values truth over tribal loyalty; educates others without condescension.

Societal Outcome (100%): Educated, thoughtful discourse. But potential paralysis by analysis — too much deliberation, not enough action.

Measurement: Questions about intellectual honesty (Q12, Q22, Q24), evidence-seeking (Q5, Q14, Q23), and principled stands (Q1, Q4, Q19).

4.3 The Skeptic

Core Traits: Critical thinking, questioning, independent, but potentially cynical.

Behavioral Signature: Questions authority and conventional wisdom; identifies flaws in arguments; resists tribal pressure; but may lack constructive alternatives.

Societal Outcome (100%): Critical thinking culture, but potential cynicism and inaction. Good at identifying problems, less good at building solutions.

Measurement: Questions about critical thinking (Q5, Q7, Q17), independence (Q11, Q13, Q20), and constructive vs. destructive criticism (Q9, Q15, Q25).

4.4 The Opportunist

Core Traits: Self-serving, extractive, zero-sum thinking.

Behavioral Signature: Prioritizes personal gain over collective good; exploits ambiguity for personal benefit; justifies selfishness through rationalization; views others as means to ends.

Societal Outcome (100%): Extractive society. Corruption, exploitation, zero-sum competition. No trust, high transaction costs.

Measurement: Questions about integrity (Q3, Q8, Q16), fairness (Q2, Q13, Q18), and self-serving justifications (Q6, Q12, Q21).

4.5 The Intellectual

Core Traits: High knowledge, low truthfulness, low accountability.

Behavioral Signature: Uses education to construct sophisticated rationalizations; defends wrong positions with "nuance" and "context"; criticizes successful people while being unsuccessful; values appearing smart over being honest.

Societal Outcome (100%): Educated corruption. Sophisticated justifications for dishonesty, intellectual cover for oppression, "nuanced" defenses of bigotry.

Measurement: Questions about intellectual honesty (Q6, Q22, Q24), sophisticated rationalization (Q1, Q2, Q4, Q5, Q8, Q12, Q16, Q17), and economic hypocrisy (Q7).

5. Societal Outcome Model

5.1 The Distribution Problem

No society consists entirely of one archetype. The critical question is: what distribution of archetypes produces what kind of society? This section presents our Societal Outcome Model, which maps archetype distributions to predicted societal states.

We define five key societal metrics:

  • Institutional Quality (IQ): Strength of merit-based systems, rule of law, and functional infrastructure (0–100)
  • Trust Index (TI): Interpersonal and institutional trust levels (0–100)
  • Innovation Rate (IR): Pace of technological and social innovation (0–100)
  • Corruption Index (CI): Prevalence of extractive behavior (0–100, higher = worse)
  • Social Cohesion (SC): Degree of cooperation vs. fragmentation (0–100)

5.5 Critical Thresholds

Our modeling reveals several critical thresholds:

The Builder Threshold (≥40%): Societies with fewer than 40% Builders cannot sustain institutional quality above "Stagnant" levels. Builders are the engine of institutional creation and maintenance.

The Intellectual Danger Zone (≥20%): When Intellectuals exceed 20% of the population, their rationalizations begin to dominate public discourse, making corruption socially acceptable. This is the "educated corruption" trap — the society has enough smart people to justify everything and not enough honest people to stop it.

The Opportunist Collapse Point (≥25%): Above 25% Opportunists, trust collapses below the threshold needed for economic transactions. Societies enter a "low trust equilibrium" where everyone must assume everyone else is corrupt.

The Sage Insufficiency: Even at 100%, Sages cannot produce a "Thriving" society alone. They lack the bias toward action that Builders provide. Sages are necessary but insufficient — they need Builders to translate knowledge into infrastructure.

The Skeptic Paradox: Skeptics are essential for accountability (preventing Builder overreach and Intellectual rationalization) but cannot build. At high concentrations, they produce a society of critics with no builders — all diagnosis, no treatment.

5.6 The Nigerian Diagnosis

Our estimated current distribution for Nigeria (40% Builder, 20% Sage, 10% Skeptic, 15% Opportunist, 15% Intellectual) produces a composite score placing it in the Extractive state. The critical finding is not the 15% Opportunists — it is the 15% Intellectuals who provide the narrative infrastructure for extraction. They make corruption intellectually respectable, defending ethnic hiring as "affirmative action," criticizing successful builders as "lucky," and using their education to construct sophisticated justifications for the status quo.

The path to "Developing" requires shifting the distribution to approximately 50% Builder, 20% Sage, 10% Skeptic, 10% Opportunist, 10% Intellectual. This is achievable through: (1) celebrating builders over critics, (2) holding intellectuals accountable for the real-world consequences of their arguments, and (3) creating systems that reward action over rhetoric.

7. Limitations and Future Work

7.1 Current Limitations

First, self-report bias: despite design efforts to minimize social desirability bias, self-report questionnaires are inherently limited by introspective accuracy and impression management. Second, cultural specificity: questions are grounded in Nigerian context, which may limit cross-cultural applicability, though the underlying psychological mechanisms are universal. Third, archetype purity: real people are blends of archetypes; the classification algorithm forces discrete categories. Fourth, temporal stability: personality traits are relatively stable, but situational factors influence behavior; a single assessment captures behavioral tendencies, not fixed identity. Fifth, the societal outcome model is theoretical — the per-capita contributions in Table 2 are estimated based on behavioral theory and qualitative analysis, not empirical calibration.

7.2 Future Work

Future directions include: (1) longitudinal validation tracking whether archetype predictions correlate with actual behavior over time; (2) peer validation comparing self-reported archetype with peer-reported archetype; (3) behavioral experiments supplementing self-report with actual behavioral tasks (dictator game, public goods game); (4) cross-cultural adaptation of questions for other contexts; (5) machine learning to predict archetype membership with higher accuracy; and (6) empirical calibration of the Societal Outcome Model using cross-national data on institutional quality, trust, and corruption.

8. Conclusion

Olodo Uprising is not a personality test. It is a behavioral simulation that asks: "If we replicated you 10,000 times, what kind of society would emerge?" The methodology draws on five decades of research in personality psychology, moral foundations theory, behavioral economics, and social identity theory. The questions are designed to bypass impression management, force genuine trade-offs, and reveal the behavioral patterns that determine societal outcomes.

The Societal Outcome Model reveals a critical insight: the most dangerous archetype is not the Opportunist (who operates transparently) but the Intellectual (who makes corruption respectable). Nigeria's current trajectory is not determined by its 15% of outright corrupt actors but by its 15% of educated rationalizers who provide the narrative infrastructure for extraction.

The goal is not to label people, but to illuminate the connection between individual behavior and collective outcomes. By understanding our own archetypal tendencies, we can make more conscious choices about the kind of society we want to build. In a country like Nigeria, where ethnic and religious divisions have been exploited by elites, where corruption has become normalized, and where educated professionals often serve extraction rather than creation, tools like Olodo Uprising are essential. They help us see ourselves clearly — not as we wish to be, but as we actually are. And only by seeing ourselves clearly can we begin to build the society we deserve.
